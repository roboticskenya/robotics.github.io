Building the Robot

Now, building the robot can be a really daunting task for anyone. My advice, give the students enough time including evennings and maybe one of the weekends. Robotics is a creative field where they will be required to think creatively and solve unique problems through their own innitiative. For that, they need enough uninterrupted hours in order to get into one of those "flow" states of work when the brain is at the most concentration.

But that is not all, you need to constantly check on them just to make sure they are not stuck because of some simple procurement staff like purchasing a few LEDs for making a sensor or because they don't have a lunch program :)

Walking into the room / workshop a few times a day even when uncalled for can be very encouraging to the students. At least you will get to hear about one or two things that did not go right, and of course you won't miss the success stories, even if tomorrow everything breaks down again.

Whenever I visit the Kisumu Poly robotics workshop during the robotics season, it usually strikes me as a beehive of activities.

Building the Chasis

The chasis is one of the first components to be bulit for the robot. We all sat down and thought about our design, put it on paper and sent the proposed design to our lecturer Mr. Ochieng Odek. We have to state clearly what we will require for the design to work out as expected and the hours needed to do the work.

With Abner leading the Mechanical team I was confident of a good progress. Soon the fruits of their hard labour started showing up. They first arrived with wheels mounted on the motors. Then with a complete chasis and the wheels mounted. This was spectacular.

It is now time for the electrical team to wire the chasis motors, mount the arduino board and the battery, and get ready for some testing.

And with that, we launched the "Waya Monster Robot" on a successful mission through the regionals to the national championships. The name "Waya" was inspired by the institution's former Principal Mrs. Joyce Nyanjom (Commonly called "Waya") by the students. "Waya" is a Kenyan Luo word for "Auntie".

The next one or two weeks will be intense days of testing and development; making new components, mounting and testing them, as we build the programming logics that will execute the tasks of the game.

Interesting things are ahead, stay tuned.
